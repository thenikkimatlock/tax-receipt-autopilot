# IRS Expense Categories Reference

> Source: IRS Publication 334 (Tax Guide for Small Business), IRS Publication 463 (Travel, Gift, and Car Expenses), Schedule C instructions. Note: IRS Publication 535 (Business Expenses) was discontinued after the 2022 tax year. For 2023+, see irs.gov/forms-pubs/guide-to-business-expense-resources.
> Last updated reference: 2025 tax year (filed 2026). Reflects OBBBA changes (signed July 4, 2025) where applicable.
> Note: These rules reflect federal tax law. State tax treatment may differ — verify with your accountant.

---

## Deductible Business Expense Categories

### software_subscriptions
**IRS basis**: Schedule C, Line 18 (Office Expense) or Line 27a (Other Expenses)
**Deductible**: 100% if used exclusively for business
**Examples**: Canva, Adobe CC, Notion, Slack, Zoom, Google Workspace, QuickBooks, Claude API, ChatGPT Plus (business use)
**Edge cases**:
- If used for both personal and business, only deduct the business-use percentage
- Annual subscriptions: deduct in the year paid (cash basis taxpayers)

---

### marketing_advertising
**IRS basis**: Schedule C, Line 8
**Deductible**: 100%
**Examples**: Meta Ads, Google Ads, TikTok Ads, LinkedIn Ads, sponsored posts, PR agency fees, logo design, copywriting for ads
**Edge cases**:
- Political advertising is NOT deductible
- Must be ordinary and necessary for the business

---

### office_supplies
**IRS basis**: Schedule C, Line 18
**Deductible**: 100%
**Examples**: Paper, pens, printer ink, folders, notebooks, small desk accessories
**Edge cases**:
- Items costing more than $2,500 may need to be capitalized as equipment
- Must be consumed or used in the current year

---

### equipment
**IRS basis**: Schedule C, Line 13 (Depreciation) or Section 179 election
**Deductible**: Full cost in year purchased via Section 179 or bonus depreciation, OR depreciated over useful life (MACRS)
**Examples**: Computers, laptops, monitors, cameras, hard drives, microphones, printers
**Edge cases**:
- Items $2,500 or under: can be expensed immediately under the de minimis safe harbor (no capitalization required)
- Items over $2,500: must be capitalized; three options — (1) Section 179 full expensing, (2) bonus depreciation, (3) MACRS depreciation over useful life — accountant review required
- **Section 179** (2025 limit: $2,500,000): elect to expense the full cost of a capitalized item in the year placed in service
- **Bonus depreciation** (OBBBA 2025): 100% for equipment acquired AND placed in service after January 19, 2025; 40% for equipment acquired before that date but placed in service in 2025
- Section 179 and bonus depreciation can be combined; Section 179 must be applied first
- SUV Section 179 cap: $31,300 for vehicles over 6,000 lbs gross weight placed in service in 2025
- Mixed-use (personal + business): only business-use % is deductible
- Must keep records of business-use percentage

---

### travel
**IRS basis**: Schedule C, Line 24a
**Deductible**: 100% for ordinary and necessary business travel
**Examples**: Flights, hotels, rental cars, Uber/Lyft to meetings, parking, tolls
**Edge cases**:
- Commuting (home to regular workplace) is NOT deductible
- Combined business/personal trips: only the business portion is deductible
- Must document: business purpose, destination, dates, business relationship

---

### vehicle_mileage
**IRS basis**: Schedule C, Line 9 (Car and Truck Expenses); Part IV of Schedule C required for vehicle information
**Deductible**: Standard mileage rate × business miles driven
- **2025 rate: 70 cents/mile** (IRS IR-2024-312)
- **2026 rate: 72.5 cents/mile** (IRS announcement December 29, 2025, up 2.5¢)
- For future years: verify the current rate at irs.gov/tax-professionals/standard-mileage-rates each January
**Examples**: Driving to client meetings, driving to a coworking space that is not your regular workplace, driving to a post office for business shipments, driving between two business locations
**Required documentation** (IRS requires ALL of the following for a mileage log):
- Date of each trip
- Starting and ending locations
- Business purpose of the trip
- Odometer reading at start and end (or total miles per trip)
- Business miles vs. total miles for the year
**Edge cases**:
- Commuting (home to regular workplace) is NEVER deductible — even under the standard mileage rate
- Cannot use standard mileage rate AND claim actual vehicle expenses (gas, insurance, depreciation) for the same vehicle in the same year — choose one method
- If you used actual expenses for a vehicle in any prior year, you cannot switch to standard mileage for that vehicle
- If you claimed Section 179 or bonus depreciation for the vehicle, you cannot use the standard mileage rate
- Standard mileage rate covers all operating costs — gas purchases are NOT separately deductible if using this method (see ocr-tips.md gas station receipt note)
- For leased vehicles: if you use the standard mileage rate, you must use it for the entire lease period
- Business use must be more than 50% to use standard mileage rate; if under 50%, actual expenses prorated for business use
- Parking fees and tolls paid for business are deductible IN ADDITION to the standard mileage rate (enter on Line 9 separately)
- Note: this is a federal deduction. State mileage rules may differ — verify with your accountant.

---

### meals_entertainment
**IRS basis**: Schedule C, Line 24b
**Deductible**: 50% only (Tax Cuts and Jobs Act limitation)
**Examples**: Business meals with clients, team lunches, meals while traveling for business
**Default classification for food vendors: `personal`** — most restaurant and food charges are personal expenses. Only reclassify to `meals_entertainment` when the user explicitly confirms all three of the following:
1. The meal involved another specific person (not a solo lunch or coffee run)
2. There was a clear business purpose (not just "I was working")
3. The primary purpose was business discussion — not a social or personal meal that happened to include work talk
**What does NOT qualify**:
- Solo meals (working lunch alone, coffee while answering emails)
- Zoom/virtual meetings (no food consumed together at a meal)
- Vague "it was kind of for business" claims
- Personal meals during a business trip (travel portion only is deductible under `travel`)
**Edge cases**:
- Entertainment (concerts, sporting events) is generally NOT deductible post-2018 TCJA
- Must document: business purpose, who was present, business relationship
- Lavish or extravagant meals may be disallowed
- If confirmed business: always note "50% deductible. Business purpose: [X]. Attendees: [X]."

---

### professional_services
**IRS basis**: Schedule C, Line 17 (Legal/Professional) or Line 27a
**Deductible**: 100%
**Examples**: CPA/accountant fees (for business), attorney fees, business consultants, bookkeeper
**Edge cases**:
- Tax preparation fees for personal returns are no longer deductible (post-TCJA 2018)
- Tax prep for your business Schedule C IS deductible

---

### utilities
**IRS basis**: Schedule C, Line 25
**Deductible**: Business-use percentage only
**Examples**: Internet service, dedicated business phone line, electricity for office
**Edge cases**:
- Home internet: only the business-use percentage (e.g., 60% business = 60% deductible)
- Personal cell phone: only the business-use percentage
- Must document business-use percentage calculation

---

### rent_lease
**IRS basis**: Schedule C, Line 20b
**Deductible**: 100% for business premises
**Examples**: Office rent, coworking space (WeWork, Regus), storage unit for business inventory
**Edge cases**:
- Home office deduction: separate rules apply (see IRS Form 8829)
- Must be for business use only (or prorated for home office)

---

### education_training
**IRS basis**: Schedule C, Line 27a (Other Expenses)
**Deductible**: 100% if related to current business/trade
**Examples**: Online courses, industry conferences, professional books, certifications, webinars
**Edge cases**:
- Must maintain or improve skills required in your CURRENT business
- Education to qualify for a NEW career is NOT deductible
- General education (not tied to current work) is NOT deductible

---

### contractor_payments
**IRS basis**: Schedule C, Line 11 (Contract Labor)
**Deductible**: 100%
**Examples**: Freelancers, 1099 workers, agencies paid for services
**Edge cases**:
- **1099-NEC threshold by tax year:**
  - 2025 payments: $600+ to a single contractor triggers a 1099-NEC requirement
  - 2026 payments (and forward): threshold rises to **$2,000** (OBBBA change) — flag payments approaching $2,000 for current-year tracking
- Must have a W-9 on file for each contractor regardless of payment amount
- Flag any contractor approaching the applicable threshold for the year being tracked

---

### insurance
**IRS basis**: Schedule C, Line 15
**Deductible**: 100% for business insurance
**Examples**: General liability insurance, professional liability (E&O), business property insurance
**Edge cases**:
- **Self-employed health insurance: deducts on Schedule 1, Line 17 — NOT Schedule C.** If the user logs a health, dental, or vision insurance payment, flag it with `review_required: true` and note: "Self-employed health insurance — deduct on Schedule 1 Line 17, not Schedule C. Not included in Schedule C total." Do NOT categorize it as a Schedule C deduction.
- Car insurance: only the business-use percentage (and only if using the actual expense method — not deductible separately if using standard mileage rate)
- Life insurance premiums: generally NOT deductible as a business expense

---

### bank_fees
**IRS basis**: Schedule C, Line 27a (Other Expenses)
**Deductible**: 100% for business accounts
**Examples**: Monthly account fees, wire transfer fees, payment processing fees (Stripe, PayPal)
**Edge cases**:
- Must be from a business account or demonstrably for business transactions

---

### home_office
**IRS basis**: Form 8829 (attached to Schedule C)
**Deductible**: Percentage of home expenses based on office square footage / total home sq ft
**Examples**: Portion of mortgage/rent, utilities, repairs, internet
**Edge cases**:
- Space must be used REGULARLY and EXCLUSIVELY for business
- Simplified method: $5/sq ft (up to 300 sq ft = $1,500 max)
- Regular method: actual expenses × business-use percentage
- Flag all home office claims for accountant review

---

### other_business
**IRS basis**: Schedule C, Line 27a (Other Expenses)
**Deductible**: 100% for ordinary and necessary expenses not covered by a more specific category
**Examples**: Business gifts, postage/shipping for business orders, professional dues and memberships, small business-related expenses that don't fit elsewhere
**Edge cases**:
- Always prefer a more specific category (e.g. `software_subscriptions`, `marketing_advertising`) when one fits — use `other_business` only as a catch-all
- Business gifts: IRS caps the deduction at **$25 per recipient per year** (any excess is non-deductible)
- Professional memberships/dues: 100% deductible if required for the business; confirm if there is a personal benefit component
- If an expense in this category later warrants its own line item on Schedule C, flag it for accountant review

---

## Non-Deductible / Personal Categories

### personal
- Groceries (unless clearly a business meal with documentation)
- Clothing (unless a uniform or required protective gear)
- Commuting costs
- Personal entertainment
- Personal travel add-ons on business trips
- Gifts over $25 per recipient per year (excess is non-deductible)
- Fines and penalties
- Political contributions
- Lobbying expenses

## Special Flags

| Situation | Required Action |
|---|---|
| Equipment purchase > $2,500 | Flag: "Must be capitalized — may qualify for Section 179 full expensing or MACRS depreciation. Accountant review required." |
| Contractor paid > $500 YTD | Flag: "Approaching 1099-NEC threshold — confirm W-9 on file" |
| Meal expense | Always note: "50% deductible limitation applies" |
| Home office claimed | Flag: "Form 8829 required — confirm exclusive/regular use" |
| Mixed-use asset | Note: "Business-use percentage needed for accurate deduction" |
| Cash transaction > $10,000 | Flag: "Form 8300 may be required" |
| Self-employed health/dental/vision insurance payment | Flag: "Self-employed health insurance — deduct on Schedule 1 Line 17, NOT Schedule C. Flag for accountant." Set review_required: true. |
| Any deductible amount stated | Add: "These are federal deductions. State tax treatment may differ — verify with your accountant." |

## Classification Defaults (Decision Tree)

Apply in order. Stop at first match.

```
1. Clearly personal? → personal, deductible: false

2. Known vendor in KB? → apply stored defaults (confirm context fit)

3. Food/restaurant vendor (Starbucks, McDonald's, Chipotle, etc.)?
   → DEFAULT: personal, deductible: false
   → ONLY reclassify to meals_entertainment if user explicitly confirms ALL three:
       a. Another specific person was present (not solo)
       b. Clear business purpose (not "I was working")
       c. Primary purpose was business discussion
   → If confirmed: meals_entertainment, deductible: true, deductible_pct: 50
       Notes: "50% deductible. Business purpose: [X]. Attendees: [X]."
       NEVER fill [X] with guesses — ask the user
   → If unsure → ask before classifying

4. Equipment over $2,500?
   → equipment, review_required: true
   → Notes: "Must be capitalized. Options: Section 179 expensing, bonus depreciation
     (100% for 2025 acquisitions post-Jan 19), or MACRS — accountant review required"

5. Contractor payment?
   → contractor_payments
   → Threshold: 2025 payments ≥ $600 triggers 1099-NEC; 2026+ payments ≥ $2,000 (OBBBA)
   → Check YTD total for this vendor; warn at 80% of threshold — confirm W-9 on file

6. Vehicle mileage (personal car for business)?
   → vehicle_mileage, deductible: true
   → Ask: miles driven + standard mileage or actual expenses?
   → CANNOT claim mileage AND separately deduct gas/insurance for the same vehicle/year

7. Health/dental/vision insurance premium for owner?
   → category: insurance, review_required: true
   → Notes: "Self-employed health insurance — deduct on Schedule 1 Line 17, NOT Schedule C."
   → Do NOT include in Schedule C deduction total

8. Legitimate business expense that doesn't fit above?
   → other_business, deductible: true
   → Business gifts: $25/recipient/year max — set deductible_pct to (25 / gift_amount × 100)

9. Purpose unclear, mixed, or ambiguous?
   → review_required: true, review_reason: [specific explanation]
   → NEVER guess a category for ambiguous transactions
```

**`deductible_pct` rules (always an integer 0–100, never a string):**
- Fully deductible: `100`
- Meals/entertainment: `50`
- Phone/internet at 60% business use: `60`
- Business gift over $25: `int(25 / total_amount * 100)`
- Unknown %: `0` + `review_required: true` + `review_reason: "Confirm business-use %"`
